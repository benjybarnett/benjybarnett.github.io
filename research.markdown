      <br><br><br><br>      <h1><b>Virtual Reality</b></h1>
      </div>
      
      <div style="text-align: left; font-size: 11.8px; padding-left: 300px; margin-top: 0%">

      	I've run a variety of projects using Virtual Reality in the Sackler Centre for Consciousness Science. In these projects, I explored topics such as embodiment, agency, and predictive perception.
      	Most recently, we <a href="/images/MSc_thesis.pdf" download="virtual_reality_deep_dream">
combined deep dream algorithms in DNNs </a>with a real-time 360&deg; VR set up with the aim of investigating the effects of
      	naturalistic hallucination-like visual input on visual perception. Specifically, we wanted to see whether such stimuli would 
      	reduce participants' reliance on sensory input and increase the weight of their priors in a precision-weighted predictive coding model of visual perception. 
      	<br><br>
      	In a more methodological aspect of the research, we explored whether using Subsitutional Reality (rather than vanilla VR) increased participants' Sense of Presence 
      	in VR headsets.

      <br><br><br><br>      <h1><b>Stereotypic Vision</b></h1>
      </div>
      
      <div style="text-align: left; font-size: 11.8px ; padding-left: 305px">
      	In a recent fMRI MVPA study in the Social and Cognitive Neural Sciences Lab at NYU, we looked for top-down effects of gender stereotypes on face perception in masked and unmasked faces. 
        Combining representational similarity analyses, functional connectivity measures, and multilevel modelling, we replicated <a href="https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5daf65330e17a4220c7707ce/t/5dbf93a6bcfc042fbcd32067/1572836262909/neural-pattern-similarity-reveals-the-inherent-intersection-of-social-categories.pdf">previous findings</a> that suggest the conceptual stereotypes stored
        in the OFC feedback to modify early represenations of faces in the fusiform gyrus in a stereotype-consistent manner. 
        <br><br>
        We extended this through the use of a backward masking paradigm, to see if
        these top-down effects of stereotypes on early visual perception could be sustained when participants did not report seeing faces. As expected, backward masking prevented representations of faces in the
        ventral temporal cortex from being modified by the stereotypes an indivdual held.
        We interpereted these with respect to <a href="https://static1.squarespace.com/static/5daf65330e17a4220c7707ce/t/5dbf8c9188977b62a39054d5/1572834454299/a-dynamic-interactive-theory-of-person-construal.pdf"> the Dynamic Interactive model </a> and <a href="http://www.scholarpedia.org/article/Global_workspace_theory"> the Global Workspace
        Theory </a> to conclude that real-time feedback from frontal regions to the ventral stream is necessary for conceptual knowledge to modify early visual representations. 
    </div>
